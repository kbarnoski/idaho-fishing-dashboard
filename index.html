Launch–11:00Rig & bank-pound
Start big and tight to the bank. On a salmonfly/golden float, lead with a Salmonfly / Chubby Chernobyl #6–8 slapped into the bank seams, foam lines and bank-side rocks. Drop a Golden Stone / Pat's Rubber Legs #8–10 18–24" off the back to cover the fish that won't commit up top. Cool morning = the dry bite may be slow to start, so the dropper earns its keep early.
11:00–3:00Warm middle · prime stonefly
The money window on a stonefly float. Stoneflies are warmth-driven — as the day peaks, adults get active and the bank eats turn violent. Keep that Salmonfly / big Golden #6–8 hard against the grass and under overhanging willows; twitch it. Use the wind: gusts knocking adults onto the water concentrate fish on the windward bank (your Jun 16 lesson) — so fish the bank the wind is blowing onto. Add a Yellow Sally #14–16 as a second look on the softer inside seams.
3:00–6:00Afternoon · goldens + searching
Goldens linger after the salmonfly. If the salmonfly eats slow, downsize to a Golden Stone dry #8–10 or a Chubby in tan/gold and keep hunting banks and current seams. On big pushy water the fish stack on the edges and in the soft pockets behind structure — pick the boat apart slot by slot. Overcast means you can keep the dry working through the afternoon instead of waiting for evening.
6:00–darkIf you float late · caddis/PMD
Evening change-up if the float runs long. As light softens, watch the flats and tailouts for a Caddis #14–16 skate or a PMD / Rusty Spinner #16 over any rising fish. On the South Fork the caddis come off the banks at dusk. But on a big-water boat day the stonefly game is the headline — don't overthink the evening unless you see noses.
Weather rule of thumb. Gray/cool overcast (that's today) → bugs hold and the dry bite lasts longer through the middle of the day, so you're not forced to wait for last light. Stoneflies are warmth-driven — they fire mid-afternoon as the day peaks, so the 11–3 window is your prime even under clouds. Wind is the wildcard on a stonefly float: it piles adults onto the windward bank and concentrates fish there (fish that bank), but it punishes the cast and mend — keep casts short, tight, and aimed at the bank, and let the boat do the work. If it truly howls, nymph the dropper through it.
