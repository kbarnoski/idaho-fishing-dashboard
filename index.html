12:00–3:00Midday · skip / nymph
Subsurface only. Full sun, building wind, zero surface game — a window to stay at the desk. If you sneak out, dredge a Pat's Rubberlegs #8 + Zebra Midge #20 dropper through the deep riffle troughs. Don't burn dries into a bright, choppy surface.
3:00–6:30Afternoon warm window · windy
Golden stone / Yellow Sallie — searching, against the wind. The 81°F peak is when warmth-driven stoneflies are most active, so a golden-stone foam or Yellow Stimulator #10–12 is the right searching bug — but 18–20 mph wind is chewing up the surface and your drift. Tuck into wind-sheltered banks and inside seams; otherwise keep the golden-stone nymph deep and bide your time. A big foam skated tight to a sheltered bank can still draw a warmth-charged brown. (No dated report has adults emerging — fish these as attractors.)
6:30–8:00Early evening · wind still up
Prospect & pre-position. Sun's still on the water and the wind is near its peak — not the BWO night a cloudy day would give you. Cover seams with a PMD Sparkle Dun #16 (PMDs building lower-river per FFF Jun 14) and a searching Parachute Adams #14. The real job this hour is positioning: get downstream of the Bill's Hole pods so you're set when the wind lays down.
8:00–8:45Prime · wind laying down
PMD spinner + caddis stacking. The wind finally eases (toward ~10) and the surface settles — watch for a Rusty Spinner #16 fall and the first caddis stacking over the pods. On a bluebird night this window opens a touch later than under cloud, so don't leave early; it builds right as the light goes warm.
8:45–dark (~9:30)Money window
Caddis blizzard — biggest fish. The take that's produced all trip, and tonight the wind has quit (~8 mph). Elk Hair Caddis #14–16 or X-Caddis #16, with a caddis emerger ~1 ft off the back if they short-take (Jun 7 lesson). Clear sky means the last light fades fast and the bite compresses — hi-vis post so you can track the fly into the dark, and fish hard till you can't see.
Weather rule of thumb. Gray/cool day → bugs come early and the dry bite holds longer. Hot/sunny → action jams into the last 2 hours of light. Stoneflies are warmth-driven — goldens/sallies fire mid-afternoon on warm days. Wind kills the dry bite — nymph through it and wait for the calm. Today is a clean bluebird example: no cloud to switch on a Baetis window and wind suppressing the surface all afternoon, so grind subsurface and search foam early — then pour everything into the last 45–60 minutes, because the wind dropping at dusk is the one thing that hands you a true caddis evening tonight.
